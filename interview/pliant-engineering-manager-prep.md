### Q4: "How do you approach leading and developing engineers?"

>
> **Practically, this means:**

> **1. Creating psychological safety.** At Seven.One, I established blameless postmortems. When we have incidents, we focus on systems, not individuals. This transformed how openly people discussed problems.

> **3. Building culture, not just process.** I created 'Lean Friday'‚Äîone Friday per sprint where anyone can work on any technical improvement. This produced breakthrough tools that increased productivity by orders of magnitude. It also showed the team their ideas matter.

> **4. DDD for shared understanding.** I introduced Domain-Driven Design not just as architecture, but as a communication tool. It cut our onboarding time from 6 to 3 months because new engineers could understand the domain through ubiquitous language."

### Q5: "Describe a challenging technical decision you made"

> "At Samdock, we were building a CRM platform and I advocated for event sourcing with CQRS‚Äîwhich the team was skeptical about.
>
> **The challenge:** Event sourcing adds complexity. Some team members worried about the learning curve and operational overhead.
>
> **My approach:**
> 1. I led event storming workshops with stakeholders to show how naturally CRM domains map to events (contact created, deal updated, etc.)
> 2. I built a proof-of-concept demonstrating how event replay would give us audit trails and temporal queries for free
> 3. I was transparent about trade-offs: eventual consistency requires careful handling, projection rebuilds need planning
>
> **The result:** We shipped to 25 production tenants. The audit trail became a selling point for enterprise customers. And when we had bugs, being able to replay events to see exactly what happened was invaluable for debugging.
>
> **What I learned:** Technical leadership is about bringing people along. The architecture was right, but getting buy-in through demonstration and transparency was just as important."
