## Category Signature
This category behaves less like a pure executive Head of AI market and more like a remote AI platform architect/player-coach market that rewards proof of production architecture, delivery, reliability, and business-to-technical translation.

Distinctive signals:
- Architecture proof is central: platform design appears in 66.3% of the sample, and deep analysis most often valued delivery (20 mentions), architecture (17), scale (15), and reliability (13).
- The role mix is hands-on and scope-led rather than org-led: senior IC accounts for 47/83 (56.6%), IC scope 45/83 (54.2%), player-coach 11/83 (13.3%), while executive scope is only 3/83 (3.6%).
- AI-specific differentiation comes from productionization, not tool breadth: agents/orchestration shows up in 16/83 (19.3%), evaluation in 11/83 (13.3%), guardrails/governance in 8/83 (9.6%), and no hard skill reaches 60%; Python is the top required language at 38/83 (45.8%).
Citations:
- Platform design: 66.3% of sample; valued evidence types in deep analysis: delivery 20, architecture 17, scale 15, reliability 13.
- Role scope distribution: IC 45/83 (54.2%), manager 23/83 (27.7%), player-coach 11/83 (13.3%), executive 3/83 (3.6%), director 1/83 (1.2%).
- AI stack signals: agents/orchestration 16/83 (19.3%), evaluation/quality 11/83 (13.3%), guardrails/governance 8/83 (9.6%); required language: Python 38/83 (45.8%).

## Headline Pattern
- Recommended structure: [Actual title or conservative family] | AI platform / systems architecture | delivery, reliability, and production AI outcomes
- Safe title families: Technical Lead, AI Platform Lead, AI Systems Architect, Principal AI Engineer, Engineering Manager, AI Platform
- Safe title variants: Technical Lead, AI Platform, AI Platform Architect, Principal Engineer, AI Systems, AI Systems Lead
- Avoid title variants: Head of AI, VP of AI, Chief AI Officer, React Leader, DevOps/SRE Executive
Evidence-first rules:
- Use the employer-given title in work history; use the headline to clarify AI platform scope only when the CV shows concrete platform, delivery, or system ownership.
- If the record is primarily IC or player-coach, prefer Architect, Technical Lead, or Principal framing over executive labels.
- Name concrete proof instead of abstract seniority: users, services, launches, reliability gains, evaluation quality, guardrails, or roadmap delivery.
- Mention cloud, Kubernetes, GitOps, Terraform, or Helm only when the CV shows direct delivery evidence.
Citations:
- Noisy title mode is true; top literal variants are inconsistent with the category label.
- Role scope skews IC/player-coach: IC 45/83 (54.2%), player-coach 11/83 (13.3%), executive 3/83 (3.6%).
- Unsafe claims in deep analysis repeatedly flagged title inflation, cloud-tool overclaiming, and React-heavy leadership claims (2-4 mentions across 20 deep analyses).

## Tagline and Profile Angle
Positioning angles:
- Hands-on AI platform architect who translates business needs into technical execution and ships production systems.
- Player-coach delivery lead for remote, distributed product engineering with emphasis on reliability, evaluation, and guardrails.
Foreground:
- Show platform architecture, system design, and production delivery first.
- Demonstrate business-to-technical translation with named initiatives, stakeholders, and shipped outcomes.
- Quantify reliability, scaling, and operational quality in live systems.
- Show mentoring and cross-functional influence without implying formal org ownership.
Avoid:
- Pure executive narrative with budget, hiring, or org-building claims.
- Frontend-heavy React positioning unless the CV documents material frontend ownership.
- Deep cloud-administrator or DevOps-operator identity unless the CV shows direct ownership.
- Regional language or market-expansion claims unless explicitly evidenced.
Safe positioning:
- Senior technical owner for AI platform or AI-enabled product delivery.
- Architect/player-coach who stays close to system decisions and implementation quality.
- Cross-functional delivery lead in remote, distributed environments.
Unsafe positioning:
- Global AI executive with hiring and P&L ownership.
- Public-cloud, Kubernetes, GitOps, Terraform, and Helm authority without project proof.
- Regional expansion owner or language specialist without explicit evidence.
- Broad product or org builder narrative detached from shipped technical outcomes.
Citations:
- Soft skills: collaboration 57/83 (68.7%), communication 56/83 (67.5%), mentoring 27/83 (32.5%), ownership 23/83 (27.7%).
- Top pain point: translate business needs into technical execution (9 mentions); other recurring needs include scalable high-quality delivery (6) and removing blockers to keep delivery on track (3).
- Management evidence is limited: hiring 2/83 (2.4%), performance management 13/83 (15.7%), org building 14/83 (16.9%), budget/P&L 0/83 (0.0%).

## Key Achievement Archetypes
### 1. Built and scaled a production AI platform
- What it proves: Proves platform ownership, architectural judgment, live-system scale, and production AI quality controls.
- Pain points addressed: translate business needs into technical execution, deliver scalable, high-quality software solutions, rapidly expanding user base
- Metrics to include: active users or teams served, workflows, models, plugins, or services supported, latency, uptime, SLOs, or incident rate, evaluation pass rate or quality score, guardrail coverage or policy checks, adoption or usage growth
- Story format guidance: Use ARIS: state the platform scope, the architectural intervention, the operating constraints, and the quantified result. Name the production environment explicitly and show before/after scale or quality.
- Citation: Platform design appears in 66.3% of the sample; agents/orchestration appears in 16/83 (19.3%), evaluation/quality in 11/83 (13.3%), and guardrails/governance in 8/83 (9.6%); deep analysis most valued delivery (20), architecture (17), and scale (15).

### 2. Turned ambiguous business goals into a shipped roadmap
- What it proves: Proves stakeholder translation, prioritization, and cross-functional delivery under unclear requirements.
- Pain points addressed: translate business needs into technical execution, lead a new product initiative, help define the roadmap
- Metrics to include: time to launch, milestones shipped, stakeholder groups aligned, scope reduced or clarified, adoption, revenue, or conversion impact if real, delivery against target dates
- Story format guidance: Use STAR: show the business ambiguity, your translation into technical work, the delivery plan you drove, and the measured release outcome. Keep the business problem concrete.
- Citation: Top pain point was translating business needs into technical execution (9 mentions); communication appears in 56/83 (67.5%) and collaboration in 57/83 (68.7%); roadmap and finding technical solutions each appeared as recurring success metrics in deep analysis (3 mentions each).

### 3. Improved reliability and resilience of live AI or platform services
- What it proves: Proves operational maturity, service ownership, and the ability to keep production systems healthy as usage grows.
- Pain points addressed: deliver scalable, high-quality software solutions, rapidly expanding user base, remove blockers to keep delivery on track
- Metrics to include: availability or uptime, MTTR or incident count, error rate or failed request rate, throughput or traffic handled, failover or routing success, alert volume or on-call burden
- Story format guidance: Lead with the failure mode or scaling risk, then show the design or operational change and the measured reliability result. Make the live-service context explicit.
- Citation: Reliability was a top deep-analysis evidence type (13 mentions); observability through logs, metrics, and tracing appears in 11/83 (13.3%); managing, monitoring, and scaling existing services appeared in deep-analysis success metrics (5 mentions).

### 4. Delivered greenfield build plus modernization of an existing platform
- What it proves: Proves range across 0-to-1 delivery and iterative improvement, which is stronger than a CV that shows only one mode.
- Pain points addressed: lead a new product initiative, deliver scalable, high-quality software solutions, shape engineering practices
- Metrics to include: new services or capabilities launched, migration percentage completed, deployment speed or release frequency, performance or cost improvement if real, legacy surface area retired, time saved for engineering teams
- Story format guidance: Use ARIS and show both sides: what you built from scratch and what you improved or retired in the existing stack. Quantify the tradeoff or migration result.
- Citation: Architecture mix shows both greenfield and optimization in 34 roles, greenfield-only in 10, and optimization-only in 10; platform design appears in 66.3% of the sample.

### 5. Raised team effectiveness through mentoring and engineering practices
- What it proves: Proves player-coach influence, standards-setting, and delivery leverage without overstating formal management.
- Pain points addressed: rapidly expanding tech team, shape engineering practices, support engineering teams
- Metrics to include: engineers or teams mentored, review or design throughput, cycle time or lead time improvement, onboarding time reduction, standards adopted, defect reduction or quality lift
- Story format guidance: Keep the story technical and delivery-linked: show the practice you introduced, how teams used it, and what improved. Avoid implying hiring or formal performance management unless explicit.
- Citation: Mentoring appears in 27/83 (32.5%), leadership in 18/83 (21.7%), and org building in 14/83 (16.9%), but hiring is only 2/83 (2.4%) and performance management is 13/83 (15.7%).

## Role Weighting Guidance
- Highest-weight roles: Technical lead, principal, staff, or architect roles with ownership of platform design and shipped systems., AI platform or AI-enabled product roles with production users, evaluation, guardrails, or workflow orchestration., Platform reliability, gateway, or multi-service architecture roles with measurable uptime, resilience, or scale outcomes., Cross-functional delivery roles that translated business needs into implemented technical roadmaps.
Expand in work history:
- Roles where you owned architecture decisions, system boundaries, or production platform design.
- Roles where you shipped live AI capabilities and can quantify adoption, quality, guardrails, or evaluation.
- Roles where you improved reliability, scaling, observability, or service resilience under growth.
- Roles where you mentored teams, shaped engineering practices, or unblocked delivery without needing a formal manager title.
Compress in work history:
- Older roles with little platform ownership, little delivery scope, or no measurable outcomes.
- Pure people-management, customer success, or product-only roles unless they directly drove technical execution outcomes.
- Frontend-heavy or tool-specific work that does not support the AI platform, architecture, or reliability narrative.
How to frame non-AI experience:
- Translate non-AI roles into architecture, distributed systems, API/platform, reliability, and delivery evidence.
- If prior work was platform or infrastructure adjacent, name the concrete operating outcomes rather than relabeling it as AI leadership.
- Use non-AI roles to prove scale, service ownership, system integration, and stakeholder translation; do not retrofit them into executive AI titles.
