## Key Achievement Archetypes
### 1. Enterprise AI platform architecture delivered to production
- What it proves: Proves fit for the category's architect-first center of gravity: platform design, delivery, scale, and operational readiness.
- Pain points addressed: the broader organization needs reusable assets and shared best practices, product and engineering teams need architectural consistency, core architecture needs to scale while staying maintainable
- Metrics to include: active users or internal adopters, number of workflows, plugins, or integrations, time to launch or rollout speed, availability, latency, or incident reduction, adoption across teams or business units
- Story format guidance: Use STAR or ARIS. Start with platform ambiguity or fragmentation, show the architecture you defined, then quantify shipped scope, operational readiness, and usage.
- Citation: Platform design 35/35 (100.0%); deep analysis valued evidence: architecture 20/20 (100.0%), delivery 18/20 (90.0%), scale 17/20 (85.0%), reliability 15/20 (75.0%).

### 2. Modernized a complex legacy or existing architecture into a more scalable design
- What it proves: Shows the ability to improve complex existing systems, which is a repeated pain point and a strong differentiator versus greenfield-only stories.
- Pain points addressed: customers need guidance improving complex existing application architectures, core architecture needs to scale while staying maintainable, product and engineering teams need architectural consistency
- Metrics to include: reduction in failure rate or incidents, performance or latency improvement, deployment frequency or release predictability, service decomposition or integration count, maintenance burden or developer productivity gains
- Story format guidance: Frame the before-state clearly: complexity, reliability issues, or slow releases. Then show the architectural interventions and the measurable operational change.
- Citation: Greenfield split: both greenfield and optimization 16/35 (45.7%), optimization-only 5/35 (14.3%); pain point: improving complex existing application architectures 3 mentions; success metrics include predictable release pipelines and resilience.

### 3. Designed multi-cloud or cross-platform architecture with reliability and resilience controls
- What it proves: Demonstrates practical architecture judgment and operational rigor, especially valuable when paired with AI platform or API-heavy systems.
- Pain points addressed: microservices and event-driven systems need to work across cloud providers, security, compliance, and reliability must be built into the architecture, customers need help designing reliable, scalable systems
- Metrics to include: clouds or environments supported, uptime or SLO attainment, failover or recovery performance, cost efficiency or routing improvements, throughput, latency, or traffic handled
- Story format guidance: Use ARIS: state the reliability or portability risk, describe the architecture and controls, then quantify stability, recovery, latency, or cost outcomes.
- Citation: Cloud preference: multi-cloud 15/35 (42.9%), Azure 5/35 (14.3%), AWS 1/35 (2.9%); deep analysis valued evidence: reliability 15/20 (75.0%), cost 6/20 (30.0%).

### 4. Governance and safe-production mechanisms for AI systems
- What it proves: Shows maturity beyond demo-stage AI by proving safe operation, quality controls, and production guardrails when the experience is real.
- Pain points addressed: security, compliance, and reliability must be built into the architecture, the broader organization needs reusable assets and shared best practices
- Metrics to include: evaluation coverage or pass rates, guardrail rules or policy checks implemented, quality, hallucination, or defect reduction, auditability or review-cycle improvements, production incident or rollback reduction
- Story format guidance: Avoid vague 'responsible AI' language. Name the concrete mechanism used, where it sat in the architecture, and what risk or quality metric improved.
- Citation: AI stack: guardrails/governance 8/35 (22.9%), evaluation/quality 3/35 (8.6%); governance is a valued evidence type 13/20 (65.0%).

### 5. Customer-facing architecture leadership that helped win or expand work
- What it proves: Demonstrates the commercial side of the role: translating architecture into customer confidence, sales support, and delivery alignment across accounts.
- Pain points addressed: sales leadership needs support identifying strategies for growth across multiple accounts and verticals, customers need help designing reliable, scalable systems, customers need guidance improving complex existing application architectures
- Metrics to include: deals supported or influenced, accounts or verticals covered, expansion revenue or pipeline influenced, customer satisfaction or retention signal, time from discovery to proposed architecture
- Story format guidance: Use STAR with a clear customer problem, the architecture recommendation you shaped, and the commercial or customer outcome that followed.
- Citation: Pain points: sales leadership growth support 2 mentions; reliable scalable systems on MongoDB 3 mentions; improving complex existing architectures 3 mentions; success metrics include winning deals and customer success/satisfaction.
