## Category Signature
A top-third CV for this category should present a senior IC or light player-coach who ships production AI platforms and features, proves architecture and reliability outcomes, and translates cross-functional needs into scalable applied AI systems rather than pure advanced experimentation or formal people management.

Distinctive signals:
- Architecture/platform scope is the clearest category anchor: platform design appears in 34/34 roles (100%), with most roles spanning both greenfield and optimization work (28/34).
- This category is delivery-heavy and senior-IC-led: senior_ic appears in 34/34 roles, with scope split between IC (19/34) and player-coach (15/34), while hiring (3/34, 8.8%) and performance management (1/34, 2.9%) stay rare.
- GenAI emphasis is practical rather than advanced experimentation-led: evaluation/quality is stronger (15/34, 44.1%) than RAG (10/34, 29.4%), agents/orchestration (9/34, 26.5%), prompt engineering (8/34, 23.5%), or fine-tuning (4/34, 11.8%).

## Key Achievement Archetypes
### 1. Built or evolved a production AI platform used by multiple teams or workflows
- What it proves: Shows the category's core value: platform architecture plus real delivery, reuse, and adoption at senior IC scope.
- Pain points addressed: build reusable ml infrastructure that supports training, deployment, and iteration, integrate ai/ml into product or platform stacks, keep systems maintainable, scalable, and low latency at large scale
- Metrics to include: number of teams or products onboarded, throughput, requests, or user volume, deployment frequency or lead time, adoption rate or active users, latency and uptime improvements
- Story format guidance: Use ARIS: state the platform gap, the architecture you designed, the implementation scope you personally led, and quantified adoption/reliability outcomes. Keep the story centered on productionization, not abstract strategy.
- Citation: Platform design 34/34 (100%); valued evidence types architecture 20/20, delivery 20/20, reliability 18/20, scale 14/20.

### 2. Improved AI system quality with evaluation, benchmarking, or feedback loops
- What it proves: Demonstrates mature applied AI engineering and distinguishes the CV from generic GenAI delivery claims.
- Pain points addressed: make search, recommendation, or AI outputs more relevant, improve reliability and production quality, reduce regressions while iterating on AI features
- Metrics to include: quality lift, accuracy, precision/recall, or ranking gains, regression rate reduction, human review pass rate, incident reduction, time-to-detect or time-to-fix quality issues
- Story format guidance: Use STAR with a quality bottleneck as the tension point. Show the evaluation method you introduced, how it changed decisions or release safety, and the measurable quality or reliability gain.
- Citation: Evaluation_quality 15/34 (44.1%); valued evidence type evaluation 15/20; success metrics include reliability, performance, and operational excellence.

### 3. Shipped retrieval or agent workflows with safe operational behavior
- What it proves: Shows practical GenAI experience in the patterns most relevant to this category without drifting into advanced experimentation framing.
- Pain points addressed: use llms and agents to automate workflows, ground model outputs with retrieval, control failure modes, cost, and latency in AI services
- Metrics to include: task completion or automation rate, cost per request or inference spend, latency or timeout reduction, fallback rate or error rate, user adoption or productivity gains
- Story format guidance: Use ARIS and explicitly name the workflow boundary, retrieval/agent design, and the controls that made it production-safe. Tie the result to cost, latency, quality, or adoption rather than novelty.
- Citation: RAG 10/34 (29.4%), agents_orchestration 9/34 (26.5%), prompt_engineering 8/34 (23.5%), guardrails_governance 5/34 (14.7%), cost evidence 11/20.

### 4. Scaled an AI-enabled service under latency, throughput, or reliability constraints
- What it proves: Signals that the candidate can handle the production engineering side of applied AI, which is central to staff/principal hiring in this sample.
- Pain points addressed: run low-latency, high-throughput systems, move models into production search or recommendation flows, keep systems maintainable and resilient at user scale
- Metrics to include: p95/p99 latency, throughput or QPS, availability/SLO attainment, error budget or incident counts, infrastructure cost at scale
- Story format guidance: Frame the story around an operational constraint. Show the architecture or service changes you drove and end with hard reliability, latency, or scale numbers.
- Citation: Success metrics include scalability, performance, resilience, reliability, high availability, and latency optimization; valued evidence types reliability 18/20, scale 14/20, cost 11/20.

### 5. Raised team delivery standards through technical leadership and mentoring
- What it proves: Supports a player-coach profile without overclaiming formal management and aligns with the category's strong collaboration and mentoring evidence.
- Pain points addressed: align cross-functional teams on architecture choices, improve execution quality across platform and product work, help teams adopt reusable AI patterns safely
- Metrics to include: number of engineers mentored, review or delivery cycle improvements, reduction in defects or rework, adoption of standards, templates, or playbooks, cross-team delivery cadence
- Story format guidance: Use STAR with a delivery or quality coordination problem. Emphasize architecture reviews, standards, or mentoring actions and quantify the team-level effect without slipping into manager language.
- Citation: Collaboration 29/34 (85.3%), communication 23/34 (67.6%), mentoring 20/34 (58.8%), player-coach scope 15/34, org_building 8/34 (23.5%).
